About

Information theorist at
the industrial frontier

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Computer Engineering at Virginia Tech, working at the intersection of information theory, spatial statistics, and large-scale AI — with a singular question: what are the fundamental limits of how data can be compressed, learned, and reasoned about?

At Argonne National Laboratory, I translated theory into deployed practice — delivering the first rigorous compressibility bounds for HPC-grade lossy compressors, so scientists can measure not just performance, but proximity to the absolute limit of what is physically possible.

My current frontier bridges these foundations into the LLM era: using token-based rate-distortion theory to mathematically explain why scaling laws obey the power-law curves measured empirically by Kaplan and Chinchilla — and predicting optimal model size and data allocation before a single training run begins.

Before academia, I co-founded Pulse Prognostics — a formative chapter reinforcing a conviction: rigorous science and industrial impact are the same goal expressed at different timescales.

Research Areas

Three frontiers,
one theory

My work extends Shannon's classical framework into the regimes that modern computing actually inhabits — finite, parallel, heterogeneous, and operating at unprecedented scale.

01 — Compression

Scientific Lossy Compression & HPC Limits

Classical rate-distortion theory fails for petabyte HPC datasets — it assumes global stationarity and infinite blocklengths that compressors never have. I developed the first tile-aware, finite-blocklength RD framework for piecewise 2D Gaussian random fields, providing true theoretical benchmarks for compressors like SPERR and SZ across four SDRBench datasets.

02 — Scaling Laws

Token-Based RD Theory for LLM Scaling

Why do neural language models obey empirical power laws? I am building the first information-theoretic proof: treating pretraining as directed compression over tokens and using semantic RD theory to mathematically bound the loss surface that Kaplan and Chinchilla measure empirically — predicting optimal (N*, D*) before training begins.

03 — Robustness

Adversarial Robustness in Wireless Systems

When adversarial perturbations travel through an unknown wireless channel, classic attack methods break down unpredictably. CDI-aware GANs learn the joint distribution over transformed perturbations — yielding a single trained defender that neutralizes multiple attack classes simultaneously, with demonstrated gains on 5G modulation classification tasks.

Vision

A theoretical grammar
for the intelligence era

"If a foundation model trained on scientific data is a compression of an entire domain — then we need a theory of that compression as rigorous as Shannon and as practical as the supercomputer it runs on."

The exascale data crisis is not merely a storage problem. It is a signal that our theoretical frameworks have not kept pace with the machines we build. Whether it is the 20 petabytes from a NYX cosmology run or the trillion tokens consumed by a frontier language model, the same question underlies both: what are the fundamental limits?

My work answers this with mathematical precision — deriving closed-form bounds, validating them against real compressors and real datasets, and translating them into actionable design guidance for next-generation HPC and AI systems.

The goal is not incremental improvement but a new theoretical grammar for resource-constrained intelligence: principled, hardware-independent, and built to last beyond the current generation of tools.

Rigor first
Every bound is proved, every model validated against data. Theory that cannot be tested by experiment is not useful theory.
Industrial reach
From Argonne's Bebop cluster to trillion-parameter models — the work must run at scale and inform real engineering decisions.
Open science
All experimental code, datasets, and derivations released openly. Reproducibility is a scientific value, not an afterthought.
